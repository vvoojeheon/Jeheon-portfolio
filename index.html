About

How I observe cities

I do not treat cities as isolated units. I observe them as relational spaces shaped by flows of people, functions, infrastructure, and institutional decisions. My central concern is not simply whether a city grows or declines, but how selection occurs within a wider urban system and why similar places experience sharply different trajectories.

My research moves across spatial analysis, behavioral interpretation, and policy inquiry. I am particularly interested in urban networks, population mobility, spatial inequality, and the mismatch between formal planning objectives and actual territorial outcomes. In practice, this means combining GIS, Python-based analysis, and empirical case studies to explain how uneven development is reproduced across cities and regions.

PhD Research Plan

Abstract

Selection, Transition Failure, and Uneven Urban Outcomes in Mega-Regional Networks

My proposed doctoral research examines why cities with comparable accessibility, connectivity, or locational advantages do not experience similar development outcomes within mega-regional systems. Existing discussions in urban and regional studies often assume that stronger connectivity and agglomeration automatically generate shared externalities and more balanced development. However, recent empirical patterns suggest that these conditions do not translate evenly into growth, commuter inflows, or functional upgrading across cities.

This project argues that connectivity may be a necessary condition but not a sufficient mechanism. I focus on the process through which urban advantages are selectively converted into demographic, economic, and functional gains, while other cities fail to make that transition despite occupying strategic positions in the network. The study therefore shifts the analytical emphasis from static urban hierarchy to the mechanisms of selection, conversion, and transition failure.

Methodologically, the research will combine inter-city network analysis, accessibility measures, commuting and mobility data, and comparative case-based interpretation within a Korean mega-regional context. The goal is to explain how networked urban systems redistribute opportunities unevenly and to identify the institutional or structural conditions under which some cities absorb externalities while others remain bypassed. In doing so, the research seeks to contribute to debates on spatial inequality, urban restructuring, and policy intervention beyond simple growth-centered regionalism.
