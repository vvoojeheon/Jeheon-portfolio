About

I approach cities not as isolated administrative units, but as relational systems shaped by connectivity, dependence, and institutional interaction. My research begins from a simple but persistent question: why do places with similar policy support, infrastructure conditions, or accessibility often produce very different outcomes?

This question emerged through field-based work in depopulating regions, planning projects, and policy research. Across these experiences, I repeatedly observed that local trajectories cannot be explained by internal conditions alone. They are also structured by how cities are positioned within wider networks of mobility, labor, governance, and regional hierarchy.

I am particularly interested in how urban outcomes are shaped by the interaction between potential connection and realized integration. In this sense, my work examines the gap between accessibility and actual functional linkage, and asks how centrality and intergovernmental cooperation condition that relationship. Methodologically, I combine spatial analysis, mobility data, network interpretation, and policy-oriented empirical research to explain uneven regional development in a more structural way.

PhD Research

My doctoral research asks why megaregions with similar connectivity conditions do not exhibit the same degree of functional integration. Rather than assuming that stronger connection automatically produces shared growth, the project examines how connectivity, centrality, and institutional cooperation interact to generate uneven regional outcomes.

Research Direction

The project reinterprets the megaregion not as a simple connected space, but as a relational structure in which network position and governance arrangement matter. It focuses on how these structural conditions shape different levels of functional integration within Korean megaregions.

Core Question
Why do megaregions with similar connectivity conditions show different levels of functional integration?
The study asks how centrality and cooperation condition the effect of connectivity, rather than treating connection itself as a sufficient explanation.
Analytical Focus
Connectivity, centrality, and cooperation as interacting structural variables
Connectivity is treated as realized functional interaction; accessibility as potential interaction capacity; centrality as dependence on higher-order core cities; and cooperation as an institutional mechanism that organizes how interurban relations operate.
Study Area
Comparative analysis of major Korean megaregions
The dissertation compares the Seoul Capital Region, Chungcheong, Busan–Ulsan–Gyeongnam, and Jeolla, using functional rather than purely administrative regional definitions.
Method
Spatial analysis, commuting-based functional networks, cooperation indicators, and regression models
The research combines descriptive structural diagnosis with econometric modeling. It uses commuting OD flows to capture functional connectivity, accessibility measures based on intercity travel time, centrality indicators tied to dependence on Seoul and regional core cities, and weighted cooperation indicators based on formal and sustained intergovernmental collaboration.
Expected Contribution
A conditional, structure-sensitive explanation of megaregional integration
Rather than assuming that connectivity automatically leads to integration, the dissertation aims to show that integration is contingent on regional hierarchy and cooperation structure. In doing so, it seeks to refine existing megaregion theory and provide a stronger basis for regional policy design.

Publication Details

This thesis examines how a sports event can function not merely as a temporary attraction, but as a locally embedded mechanism for relational population formation, resource activation, and community coexistence. Using the Jangsu Trail Race as a case, the study analyzes how event participants encounter local resources, how peripheral regions become temporarily connected to broader mobility networks, and how an event portfolio model can support long-term local sustainability.
